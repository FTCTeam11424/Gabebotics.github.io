Lecture 1.1 - Understanding 

Logic: Three Acts
  1. Understanding (Produces Concept) - (Expressed as Term)
  2. Judging (Produces Judgement) - (Expressed as Proposition) 
  3. Reasoning (Produces Reason) - (Expressed as Argument)

Essence - That which makes a thing itself; to change the essence is to change the thing into something else.
Accidence - those qualities of a thing which can be changed without affecting the essence

Understanding - the moment of mentally grasping the essence of a thing (Distinguishing the Essence and Accidence) 
When the mind understands, it produces a mental “concept” - a non-material idea of that thing. 
This concept is unique to each individual (particular)
When we express the concept in words, we name it with a universally understood word which allows for communication. 

Lecture 1.2 - Concepts, Terms, Words

Five Characteristics of Concepts
  1. Concepts are immaterial
  2. Concepts are abstract (from abstraho, to draw out) (Rectangle example, concepts are what we develop from the rectangles) 
  3. Concepts are universal 
  4. Relations between concepts are necessary (not constructed)
  5. Concepts are unchanging (They don’t grow/mature, or decay) (OUR UNDERSTANDING CHANGES, The concept itself does not) 

Terms - in the public domain; universally means what is connoted by the specific linguistically contextualized word; can be one word or a group of words, but must be less than a sentence 
    • Love, caritas, lies, amor, amour, agape - six words, one term
    • The common term is what allows for words to convey the same meaning in different languages
  • Terms are never true or false; instead, they are ambiguous or unambiguous 
    • Ambiguous - the terms could mean more than one thing, and its usage makes the meaning unclear
    • Unambiguous - one and only one meaning is in play at a given moment 

Extension & Comprehension
  • Every term has both extension and a comprehension
  • Extension
    • All the real things the term refers to, the population of that term in the world. 
    • Ex. Triangles extend to every triangle in the world
    • Ex. The extension of ‘man’ is the 6+ billion men who exist
    • Extension is quantitative: Measurable, you can count all the individual beings the term refers to
  • Comprehension
    • The term’s inner meaning; all the meaning the term comprehends or includes within itself
    • Ex. What does everyone consider a triangle to be?
    • Ex. The comprehension of man is ‘rational animal.’
    • What does it mean to be this term?
    • Comprehension is qualitative: You cannot count a term’s internal meaning 
  • Every term has the qualities of extension and comprehension, but these qualities are inversely related - the greater the extension, the less the comprehension (and vice versa) 
    • Ex. Right Triangle - Doesn’t include acute or obtuse triangles, but we know more about the triangle (90 degree angle, etc)
    • ’Animal has greater extension than ‘man’ (because there are more animals in the world than men) but ‘man’ has greater comprehension (because man means ‘rational animal’ - it contains all that ‘animal’ means and then more)
