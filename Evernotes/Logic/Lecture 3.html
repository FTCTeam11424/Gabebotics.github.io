Fallacies of Language: 
  • Fallacy of Equivocation
    • The same term is used in two or more different ways over the argument
    • Suckers the listener with one definition and tricks him into agreeing with the second
    • “You cant take that, its stealing” “In baseball stealing is good” “You have a point there...”
    • Intentional Fallacy
  • Fallacy of Amphiboly 
    • Word order which creates a misleading meaning (bad grammar) 
    • Creates a lack of clarity, unintentional meaning
    • If the sentence structure is wrong or the meaning absurd, your dealing with Amphiboly 
    • “Don’t let worries kill you. Let the church help” (Is intended to mean Let the church help you relieve your worries, actually means let the church help kill you) 
  • Fallacy of Accent
    • An ambiguous statement which applies through accent, emphasis, tone, irony, or sarcasm, a different meaning. 
    • It communities on two levels; this fallacy is undetectable in writing, in person more is communicated than said. 
    • “Jack will cycle to the restaurant tonight.” “Jack will Cycle to the restraint tonight.” 

Fallacies of Diversion: 
  • Argument I’m Ad Hominem - against the man 
    • Attacks the person making the argument in some way instead of the argument 
    • It diverts attention from argument to the person, bringing up irrelevant information about the 
    • Three kinds of ad hominem: 
      • Poisoning the well - focuses on trustworthiness of person making the argument, trying to make him seem not credible 
      • Tu quoque - (Translates as you too!) Occurs when the response to an argument is “You do this bad thing too!” 
      • Genetic Fallacy - Discredits the person by linking the person to some known bad person/idea. Example: Bob lives next to a slaveholder!
  • Ad Verecundiam - appeal to (irrelevent) authority 
    • Occurs when there is a illegitimate appeal to authority because the authority is
      • irrelevant: Not related to the topic 
      • unreliable: Authority is not reliable (Ph.D. At diplomacy mill) 
      • unnecessary: Citing something unnecessarily.
      • dogmatic: Because someone said so, cant be argued
      • uncritical:
  • Argumentum Ad Baculum - appeal to fear 
    • Appeal to force/fear instead of reasoning; instead of giving reasons, offers a threat or possible threat. 
    • This fallacy diverts attention from argument made to fear of consequences 
    • Often when fear is used instead of a reason or piece of evidence
  • Argumentum Ad Misericordiam - appeal to pity 
    • Argument is made which appeals to audience’s pity rather than evidence. 
    • This fallacy diverts attention from argument to sad facts in order to get pity.
    • Sad facts that do connect to actual reasoning 
  • Argumentum Ad Ignominium - appeal to shame 
    • Threat of shame or embarrassment replaces reason or evidence. 
    • Shame depends on the particular group, so it is not actual reasoning but appealing to a group or person’s subjective feeling.
    • “What? You’re going to be a lawyer? Is it because you want people to tell jokes on you?”
  • Argumentum Ad Populum - Appeal to the Masses 
    • Appeals to general agreement of large numbers of people instead of actual evidence 
    • Number of people who affirm an idea has nothing to do with the truth of the idea. Populace instead of evidence 
    • “But everybody else is doing it” “40 million Frenchmen can’t be wrong” “Capital punishment can’t be wrong; 75% of the people support it!” 
  • Argumentum Ad Ignorantium - appeal to ignorance 
    • People appeal to a lack of knowledge for their conclusion, Something is true because we dont know otherwise
    • Ignorance cannot be a premise or a reason; Argues from negative stance, negative cant prove a positive 
    • “He can’t prove he earned that money, so he must have stolen it” 
    • “Aristotle? Never heard him. So he can’t be that important” 
    • “How can there be a war on? I haven’t seen any evidence of it!”
