Section 2: 

“What is truth?”
The Correspondence Theory of Truth 
  1. Truth is that which corresponds to reality 
    1. A statement is true if what it says aligns with the real events or information which exists 
    2. A thing is true if it represents itself as it really is 
  2. Aristotle’s definition of truth: 
    1. “If a man says of what is that it is, or of what is not that it is not, he speaks the truth, but if he says of what is not that it is, or of what is that it is not, he does not speak the truth.” 
      1. If someone says something exist and it does, he speaks the truth. If someone says something exists and it doesn’t, he does not speak the truth. 
      2. If someone says something doesn’t exist and it does not, he does speak the truth. If someone says that something doesn’t exist and it does, he does not speak the truth. 
  3. In the act of judging whether a proposition is true or false, we are determine whether or not the statement lines up with reality. 
According to Correspondence Theory of truth,                 Truth is not located inside one person, but describes the relationship between some thing and a OBJECTIVE reality. There is only one truth, the truth. 

Section 3: 
  1. every declarative sentence can be fit into four sentence patterns. 
  2. These patterns make it easy to see: 
    1. Subject
    2. Predicate
    3. Quantity - All or some
    4. Quality - positive or negative 
  3. Think of his as an act of translation - we are translating Norma, subjective human communication into as objective as possible form, in order to evaluate truth of proposition. 

Every proposition has:
  1. A matter - the subject term, and the predicate term
    1. What it is about, what it is judging, what it is declaring, is nearly infinite
  2. A form - the quality and quantity 
      1. Quantity: how much of a thing we are talking about
        1. Particular: some OR Universal: all
      2. Quality: whether the proposition is 
        1. Affirmed: the predicate is a affirmed of the subject
        2. Negated - the predicate is denied of the subject 

Four possibilities for logical form
A: universal affirmative propositions (All men are mortal)  
E: universal negative propositions (No men are mortal) 
I: Particular affirmative propositions (Some men are mortal) 
O: Particular negative propositions (Some men are not mortal) 

Formulas:
A - All {S} is {P} 
E - No {S} is {P} 
I - Some {S} is {P} 
O - Some {S} is not {P}

Distribution of terms: 
  • Every term (S and P) in a logical proposition is either distributed or undistributed 
  • Distributed - Claims to be universally true of all members of a class
  • Undistributed - Claims to be a particular, true of some members of a class
  • All or some shows us whether he subject term is distributed or undistributed
    • The subject of an I or O sentence is always undistributed
    • The subject of an A or E sentence is always distributed
  •  The copula (is or is not) tells us whether the predicate is distributed or undistributed 
    • The predicate of an affirmative proposition is always undistributed
    • The predicate of a negative proposition is always distributed 
  • Easier Way: 
  • A: distributed undistributed 
  • E: distributed distributed
  • I: undistributed undistributed 
  • O: undistributed distributed 

Converting Propositions: 
  • Conversion: interchanging the subject and predicate 
  • Only terms with the same property of distribution can be interchanged
    • Swapping S and P in A sentences is called the Fallacy of an Illicit Conversion of an A Propositions 
  • E and I propositions can be converted validly (just swap the predicate and the subject. 
  • An A proposition converts to an I proposition 
  • O propositions do not convert `
