Roman Recap:
  • Two thousand years ago the world was ruled by Rome, but Rome could not rule itself.
  • Two men wrestled Rome back from Chaos and turn a republic into an empire
    • From 509 BC - 27 BC, Rome was a republic
    • Power lied with the Senate, and Senators were fighting for power between themselves. Order had give way to anarchy and only strength mattered.
  • Rome had expanded far beyond its original border
    • Controlled Italian peninsula, North Africa, Greece, Spain
  • Roman Legions inspires awe
    • Efficient, Builds infrastructure and connects known world
  • Romanization
    • Without war, Roman Cities spread, “Be like the Romans”
  • Many long for the “good old days,” Romans no longer live a simple life
  • The Great Urban Mob (of unemployed soldiers)
  • Wealth gap widens between patricians and Plebeians
  • Blood and fighting in the Senate

Julius Caesar
  • Backstory:
    • Born into an aristocratic family in around 100 BC, Julius Caesar grew up in dangerous times
    • The nobility were widely discredited and order gave way to chaos. The only clear alternative was military dictatorship
    • Caesar allied himself against nobility
    • As his career took off, he won a number of political offices, not always by reputable means. By 63 BC, he had become well-known, but controversial figure.
    • Appointed governor of Spain
      • Lucrative position, because he could plunder the local inhabitants at will
      • Returned to Rome in 60 BC, and the following year was elected consul

The First Triumvirate
  • Caesar, Pompey, and Crassus
  • Caesar allied himself with Pompey and Crassus
  • Pompey was a war hero under General Sulla
  • Crassus was a multimillionaire (land proprietor) also served under Sulla
  • Before this alliance, Pompey and Crassus were rivals. (Caesar bridges this gap)

Rome turns to Marius
  • Gaius Marius:
    • Roman general and statesman
    • Originally a common citizen
    • As a general he won victories in Numidia (modern Algeria) against Jugurtha
    • In 101 BC, the Cimbri invade Italy, and were defeated by Gaius Marius, and their king was killed
    • Marius is not above anything, will get down in the ditches with his soldiers. Increases # of Roman soldiers (Allows people that dont have land to fight).
      • Inspires loyalty
  • Marius held the office of consul an unprecedented amount of times during his career.
  • Legend - Marius found an eagles next with seven chicks in it as a teen. (Very rare)
  • This was believed to be an omen saying he would be consul seven times.
  • Later, he decreed that the eagle would be the symbol of the Senate and People of Rome
  • Plutarch has recorded all of these events in his collection of writings titled Lives

Crassus
  • Started his military career under Sulla
  • Interested in Real Estate
  • Crassus rose to political prominence after his victory over the slave revolt by Spartacus, sharing his consulship with Pompey the Great (his rival)
  • Following his 2nd Consulship, Crassus was appointed the governor of Roman Syria (which promised immense wealth)
  • Crassus uses Syria to launch military campaigns against the Parthian Empire. (He wanted military power like Caesar and Pompey)
When Caesar was consul:
  • Caesar wanted to pay off Pompey’s soldiers by allocating them public lands
  • This was unpopular, but he was smart and played both sides, and eventually got his way
    • Wants to gain favor of populares AND optimates
  • Caesar’s intention originally was not to attack Gual, but Rumania, which was rich in precious metals.
  • He ended up attacking Gaul because:
    • the migration of the Helvetians, a coalition of tribes in modern Switzerland, into Gaul.
    • The Helvetians crossed into Roman territories, this was unacceptable
    • For Caesar, it was a golden opportunity to impress everyone.
    • There were reports that Germans were attacking the Aedui, a tribe allied to Rome.
    • A victory would place him on the same level as his uncle Marius

Conquering Gaul
  • The Gallic Wars were a series of military campaigns led by Julius Caesar against several Gallic tribes.
  • Gaul has many different tribes, they are not united, and because of that have a weak response.
  • Caesar beat the Gallic tribes.
  • Gaul was important to the Romans because they had been attacked by several tribes there.
  • Conquering Gaul allowed Caesar to control the natural border of the river Rhine.

Sub Section: Caesar “visits” Britannia  
  • Britain:
    • Caesar invades Britain twice, in 55 and 54 BC.
    • Caesar claims that the Britons had supported the Gauls against him.
    • Caesar starts Roman exploration (there was debate whether the land mass existed)
    • The first invasion was Unsuccessful: The Romans only gained a beachhead on the coast of Kent
      • Bad rains, tides, rocky cliffs,
    • The second invasion was more successful: the Romans landed at a spot where there was little Britons. They have a diplomatic meeting with the Britons. Caesar sets up a king, Mandubracius, who was friendly to Rome
    • No territory was conquered and held for Rome
  • During the reign of Julius Caesar, had an Iron Age culture
  • Population between one and four million
  • Agriculture was being developed in Britain, and the economy was heavily divided between lowlands and highlands. Lowlands (southeast): Fertile land made farming possible
  • What Caesar Learned:
    • The Britons used chariots, which were unfamiliar to Romans, so he incorporated them.
    • Caesar wanted to learn more about the geography and culture
    • He defines Britons as typical barbarians, yet brave and if crushed they could bring glory to the Romans.
    • Caesar learned how to set up a system of client kingdoms
    • Brings Briton into political sphere of Rome
    • Trading relations increased between Rome and Britain over next century
Crassus and Parthia
  • Crassus’ campaign in Parthia was a complete failure, ending in his defeat at the Battle of Carrhae
  • Crassus’ death unravels the alliance between Pompey and Caesar
  • Within 4 years of Crassus’ death, Caesar would enter Rome and begin a civil war against Pompey and the optimates.

Juliu-Claudia Family (Family after Caesar)
  • Augustus -> from Julian line
  • Livia (his wife) -> from Claudia family
  • First five emperors use the names “Julio-Claudian”
  • Tiberius was Agustus’ heir, was Livia’s son from a previous marriage (Augustus adopted him)  

Tiberius
  • Strong military general
  • Conquest of Pannonia, Dalmatia, Raetia, and parts of Germania, laid the foundations for the northern frontier of the Roman Empire
  • He left the empire wealthy and secure, but did not name a successor
  • He is remembered as old and cranky, and not wanting to be emperor.

Caligula
  • He earned the nickname “little boots” from growing up in an army camp with his father (a commander) 
    • He was a mascot for them, they made him a little uniform
  • He is not remembered as a good emperor
  • He was a little crazy(Possibly caused by week long fever)
    • Plans to marry his sister but then kills her 
    • During a storm he instructed soldiers to “whip the waves” to punish Neptune
    • Promoted his favorite horse to consul
  • He is known for lavish parties and endless feasting
    • Spends empire’s money on himself
  • He ordered a lot of executions, some of which senators so that he could take their money. 
  • Caligula was assassinated by Cassis Chaerea (at Palatine Games 
  • Caligula’s uncle (Claudius) succeeded him 

Claudius 
  • Much better emperor than Caligula 
  • Acquired the province of Britain
  • Stabilized empire, rebuilt treasury
  • He married his niece, Agrippina (adopted her son, Nero)
  • Most people believe she poisoned him so that her son could gain power

Nero
  • Nero was the last Julio-Claudia emperor 
    • He makes sure of this by killing all of his blood relatives. 
  • Nero had “un-Roman” interests: 
    • He liked to sing and perform on stage
    • He wanted to compete in athletic events like the Olympics 
  • He was good in the first years of his reign: 
    • Forgave debt 
    • Maintained plenty of money in the treasury 
    • No Crisis developed 

Nero’s Mother
  • Nero’s mother “held him back” from a lot of his interests (discouraged him from acting, competing, etc.) 
    • He asked his tutors to assassinate her; they refused
    • He gave his mother a boat that fell apart as she sailed, but she survived. 
    • He has her executed and planted weapons on her servants to make it look like she was going to assassinate him. 

Nero’s Interests
  • Nero participated in 1700 horse racing events in one year. 
    • He always won (even the time he fell out of his chariot - the judges said “he would have won”) 
  • His singing performances would last for 12 hours at a time (Possibly not accurate) 
    • People faked death, jumped off balconies to get away from performances 
    • The next emperor, Vespasian, was exiled for falling asleep during Nero’s performance

The Great Fire of Rome 
  • In July 64 AD, a large fire destroys 70% of city of Rome 
  • It began in one apartment, spread to others, winds picked it up. 
  • Wooden houses were common
  • Imperial soldiers tried to keep the fires from spreading
    • It is rumored that Nero “fiddled while Rome burned” but there’s no evidence for this. 

Blame for the Fire
  • Nero needs a scapegoat, he blamed the Christians 
  • He prosecuted them horribly 
    • He tied them to poles and set on fire to provide light 
    • He sent them into arenas to face wild animals or armed troops 
    • This is when Peter and Paul were killed 
  • Christianity went “underground” for about 200 years.

Nero’s Golden House
  • Nero made a huge private house for himself called the “golden house” on land that was cleared by the fire. 
    • It was built near the site of the Colosseum 
    • The house is still able to be excavated today. 
  • It had retractable roofs for star-gazing
  • Hydraulics works that squirted perform
  • Couches had silk 
  • Finest artwork, mosaics, and statues. 
  • Took up huge space in downtown Rome

The End of Nero
  • Despite Nero’s efforts, he remained unpopular with the people
  • He wasted money
  • He enraged people in Rome’s provinces
  • He did not set a good example
  • The Senate declares him a public enemy 
    • He had no support, and he did not want to be exiled
  • Nero orders his slave to kill him

After Nero 
  • Senators and members of the upper class are happy that Nero died
  • Members of lower class were disappointed at lack of entertainment
  • There were mixed feelings among soldiers
  • Chaos ensued because Nero had killed all his blood relatives, and had no heir
  • Four men with armies each declared themselves emperor

Vespasian (ruled 69-79 AD) 
  • Vespasian was in Judea, besieging Jerusalem 
    • “Great Revolt” (Jewish rebellion) lasted from 66-73 AD
  • Vespasian was hailed as emperor by his soldiers, who were in Egypt and Judea 
  • Egypt was essential to Rome, and by cutting the grain supply it was easy for Vespasian to claim power. 
  • Vespasian sent armies ahead of him to secure his arrival in Rome
  • His reign begins the Flavian Dynasty 
  • He set about fixing the situation in Rome
  • He flooded Nero’s Golden House and drained the lake next to it. 
  • Vespasian did not live to see the Colosseum completed
  • Vespasian’s Legacy:
    • Restored Pax Romana
    • Well liked by people
    • Brought in lots of money
    • Emphasized financial responsibility

Sack of Jerusalem
  • While Vespasian was ruling, his son Titus carried on Vespasian’s siege of Jerusalem
  • In 70 AD, the siege ended when Titus sacked Jerusalem, which provided funds for Colosseum.
  • The Arch of Titus shows Titus’ achievements, including the Sack of Jerusalem

Construction of the Colosseum 
  • Begun under Vespasian. (Huge amphitheater that could seat 50,000)
  • Hydraulic lifts to bring up animals from underground
  • The area could be flooded for naval battles
  • Contractible canvas covers
  • This amphitheater replaced the 100 foot statue of Nero (The Colossus) 
    • Called the colosseum because it is next to the Colossus 

Titus (79-81) 
  • Only reigns for two years
  • Opened the Colosseum 
  • Both Titus and Vespasian were popular
  • During Titus’ reign, Mt. Vesuvius erupted, destroying Pompeii and Herculaneum 

Pompeii 
  • Significant trading city and agricultural center
  • Wealthy city with rich volcanic soil for growing crops
  • Resort for wealthy Romans. 
  • Serves as a “time capsule”, gives us Roman life at its finest
  • Structures like baths, temples, shops, and houses were preserved. 

Eruption of Vesuvius (79 AD) 
  • My. Vesuvius erupted in August, 79 AD
  • Pumice and ash were expelled across a wide area
  • Most of the inhabitants died in the first explosion or through heat and suffocating ash
  • The city in ash was buried over a long time, and sometimes 20 feet deep. 

Pliny the Younger
  • Witnessed the eruption of Mt. Vesuvius (Primary source) 
  • Some were able to escape the city after the first tremors, but there were not enough boats to bring everyone to safety.
  • Pliny the Elder was his father and was in command of ships near Pompeii
