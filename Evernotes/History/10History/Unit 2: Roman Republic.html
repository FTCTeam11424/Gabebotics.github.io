Geography
  • Italy’s central location was important for making money
    • collect taxes from merchants
    • Hospitality industry (Tourism, used inns, home renting)
    • Practice of Piracy(Pirates at docks, kidnapping, stealing, looting)
  • Italy’s natural resources: 
    • Grapes - wine industry was important
    • Olives - oil for lamps, skin moisturizer, used in cooking
    • Cereal grains - the Romans made bread every day
    • Metal ore 

Where the Romans Came From
  • Greeks defeat the Trojans at Troy
  • Aeneas obeys Jupiter; eventually settles in Italy
  • Values in the Aeneid: 
  • Romans came from somewhere else - they didn’t try to claim that they always ruled in Italy
  • They were conquerors - this is what they wanted to be viewed as (in their own history book)
  • The importance of agrarian society(Romans were huge farmers)
  • Value hard work
  • Pious/religiously devout people

Early Roman Values
  • Conservative people who cling to tradition 
    • Favor old/established over new/innovative (They get their norms from Greeks mostly)
    • Mos maiorum - ancestral custom or “way of the elders” 
      • This is where the Romans derived their social norms 
  • Heirarchial
    • They know where they fit into society and want to stay there and want others to stay there
    • Conquered people become slaves (not race, ethnicity) 
    • Government, family, and religion -> every aspect of life had a social ladder
    • Patriarchial - Male is head of family. If father dies, son is head of family. 
  • Interest in appearances
    • Dignitas - dignified quiet behavior was important
    • Gravitas - seriousness of purpose 

Roman Religion
  • Reciprocity of exchange between humans and deities - “I am going to give to you so that you give me what I want.”
  • Roman religion did not focus on punishment, but rather on what the gods could withhold from you if you didn’t give to them 
  • Native Roman deities were agrarian - gods of grain storage, harvest, fire 

New/Old Religious Traditions
  • Over time, Romans adopted many new gods
  • New religions were added as long as the old traditions were also kept
  • Romans believed people should be able to practice their own religion without much restriction
  • Jupiter was the “father” of the gods - he was in charge of all Roman religion 
    • Obvious hierarchy in Roman religion

 Who are these people?
  • Latins are first inhabitants of Rome
  • The Etruscans occupied the region to the north of Rome, between the Arno and Tiber Rivers, to the west of the Apennine Mountains
  • The Romans were first a subject people of the Etruscans and later their conquerors 

The Founding of Rome
  • Foundation myth: The story of Romulus and Remus is important for what it communicates about Roman character and the Roman outlook
    • Romulus + Remus are children of Mars (Leaders often claim they have divine lineage)
    • Mother transfers traits through nursing (R + R, get pack instincts, loyalty, aggression)
  • Jupiter sent his eagle to signal to Romulus where to found “his city” 
  • Remus taunts Romulus, stepping over the boundary stone Romulus set to mark his city (This was highly improper behavior) 
  • Romulus runs his sword through Remus
    • The state matters more than family (Even your own twin brother) 

The Regal Period: The Kings
  • Romulus was the first king of Rome 
    • Builds the city with his followers
    • Romulus is also portrayed as a conqueror who came from elsewhere to found Rome
  • Seven kings of Rome: 
    • First four were Latin: 
      • Romulus, Numa Pompilius, Tullius Hostilius, Ancus Marcius
    • Last three were Etruscan (Romans come to see Etruscans as foreigners to be overthrown: 
      • Tarquinius Priscus, Servius Tullius, and Tarquin Superbus (Tarquin the Proud) 
    • Little is known about the kings between Romulus and Tarquin the Proud

Tarquin the Proud
  • Extended family gave him trouble
    • His son assaulted Lucretia (“the Rape of Lucretia”)
    • She was a model of Roman Virtue 
    • She kills herself to preserve her and her family’s honor 
    • The Roman nobility storm Capitoline HIll in response to this event (509 BCE) 
    • They run Tarquin and his family out of town 
  • Declared an end to kings forever 
    • They could not even bear to hear the word “king” (“Rex”)
  • Monarchy in Rome ends 

The Roman Republic: 509 BC
  • SPQR (the Roman Senate and the People) (Brand of Roman Republic) 
    • The government was now the business of the people 
    • The people are now “king” 
  • Romans don’t sit around and think of the best form of governing (like the greeks) instead they create and adapt their government as needed
  • Their constitution is not one document, but a set of traditions

Roman Republic (509 BCE) 
  • Consuls (2) - elected anually
    • Took on the responsibilities of the king
  • The Senate rules with the consuls, holding equal power as a ruling body

The Office of Censor
  • Censors (2) - were elected every five years
  • Very highly esteemed position
  • Counted the people (conducted the census)
  • Decided how to allocate funds (based on census results)
  • Oversaw public morals - they could take away citizenship, eliminate people who were seen as “unfit” in society, discouraged improper behavior
    • This was based on the subjective judgement of the Censor 
    • This is where we get our notion of “censorship” 

How to be Elected in Rome 
  • Military reputation 
    • A man had to prove he was a “manly” man (where we get the world “virtue” today - from “vir”) 
  • Popularity
    • Men who did well in wild animal hunts
    • Men who reenacted battles in the arena (not the same extent as it will be later during the Roman Empire) 
  • Previous appointment for government office 
    • Men with government experience were often elected

Internal Conflict: Patricians & Plebians 
  • Patricians 
    • “Fathers” of the people
    • Aristocratic (wealthy, landowning) leaders of society 
    • Accustomed to advising kings as a “council of elders” or “Senate” - now they are the ones making the decisions for the people of the Republic 
    • At this time, the only members of society that can be consuls or members of the Senate
  • Plebians
    • Non-Patricians (majority of society) 
    • Working Class 
    • They believed that they should have full rights as citizens and a say in the government - sometimes this struggle became violent

Ways to Move Up in Society
  • Most Romans stayed in their particular group their entire life, but it was possible to “become” a patrician
    • The easiest way was to be adopted by a patrician - promising young plebeian boys might be adopted into patrician households 
    • Later (~445), plebians won the right to intermarry into patrician families, which provided another way to “become” a patrician

The Plebeians “Strike” 
  • 15 years after the overthrow of the monarchy, the plebeians leave town. They sit on the opposite river bank and refused to work. 
  • Without the plebeians, there was no shopkeepers, manufacturers, no foot soldiers, no one to watch or tutor the patricians’ children, etc. 
  • It is unknown how long the “strike” lasted, but it got the attention of the patricians, who were now willing to listen to the concerns of plebeians. 

Plebeian Demands
  • The plebeians formed an assembly called the “Council of Plebs” 
  • Plebeians demanded that clear laws be written down and posted - this was finally achieved in the creation of the 12 Tables in 451 BC.
  • Plebians also want representation in the Senate, so they create the office of “Plebeian Tribune”
    • Powers of the Tribune: could convene the Senate, veto any measure, or shut down the Senate for recess
    • The Tribune reported regularly to the Plebeian Assembly 
  • The plebeians also eventually won the right to appeal a sentence or join the priesthood

Plebeians’ Greatest Victory 
  • 287 BC: Plebeians won their greatest victory
  • ”Lex Hortensia” 
  • Proposed by Hortensius (Every Latin law was named after the person who proposed it in the feminine form) 
  • Lex Hortensia stated that all laws passed by the plebeians in the Plebeian Assembly apply to patricians as well
    • This equalizes laws and offices in Rome between patricians and plebeians

Progress for Plebeians
  • By 287 BC, all Roman men had access to religious and political offices
  • All Roman men could participate in assemblies
  • However, actually gaining a position in government was extremely rare and difficult for a plebeian 

Roman Expansion in Italy: 510-287 BC
  • From the birth of the Republic, Rome was expanding throughout the Italian Peninsula
  • The Etruscans were the first group conquered by the Romans - from Etruria 
    • Makes Colonization possible north of Rome 

The Etruscans 
  • The Etruscan language is not yet deciphered, but the alphabet is the same as ours 
    • Scholars are able to read names and numbers
    • Scholars can tell that it is not an Indo-European language, o the Etruscans were not native to Italy = they may have arrived the same time as the Romans
  • Most Etruscan culture is deciphered through burials 
  • The Etruscans were further advanced than the Romans in a number of ways (trade, manufacture, communication throughout the Mediterranean) 
  • Etruscans exported iron and bronze wares
    • Helmets, weapons, chariots, urns, candelabras, statues
    • They traded these for luxury items and were wealthy
  • Etruscans also made linen and woolen clothing, leathers goods, fine gold, and pottery

Fun Fact 
  • There was an over abundance of iron in Etruria, but the Etruscans were not great at extracting it
  • There was enough slag left over from Etruscan iron works to provide Mussolini with iron for weapons and tanks thousands of years later

The Etruscans
  • The Etruscans borrowed much from Greek culture and passed it on to the Romans
  • Method of fighting: the hoplite phalanx
    • Densely-packed lines of men crashing into each other with the intention of creating an opening - the successful side pours through the opening while the cavalry comes from the sides 
  • The alphabet
    • Greeks learned the alphabet from the Phoenicians (modern day Lebanon, Israel, Syria) the Etruscans learned it from the Greeks, and the Romans learned it from the Etruscans
    • Romans will spread the alphabet throughout the Western world
  • Engineering
    • The arch
    • Great sewer and hydraulics works
  • Gladiatorial Games
    • Variety in combinations was important (people v people, animals v animals, animals v people) 
      • Example: 4 lions vs. 1 tiger
  • City Planning
    • Grid-like structures used (Romans also used grid-like structures for military camps) 
  • Divination
    • ”Reading the future” by examining animal entrails 

Sabines
  • Northern society
  • Often aligned with other groups against the Romans
  • Early in Roman history, Romans would steal women from Sabine to intermarry with
  • Conquered Sabine territory and imposed conscription (a draft)
  • Romans were harsh in their rule, so the Sabines rebelled often

Samnite Wars:

Gauls

Rome recovers surprisingly quickly

Rebellions against Rome
  • From 350-338 BC Romans successfully crushed rebellions of Samnites and other groups, maintaining a strong hold of Italy
  • Brutal suppression of conquered people
  • Decimation of male populations by the Romans
    • Local men stood in line 
    • Roman soldiers counted off by tens - every 10th man was killed
    • This was in front of family members with as much gore as possible 
    • This was the best that could happen. More often, all men were killed and women/children were enslaved. 

Opposition Silenced 
  • By 338 BC, there was no organized league against the Romans
  • Individual city-states like the Samnites and Volscian (etc.) now even enjoyed a limited type of Roman citizenship
  • These groups (in time) began to think of themselves as Roman

Roman Colonization
  • Roman colonies looked a lot like Rome in terms of structure and culture
  • There were different types of colonies, depending on the type of Roman citizenship of the colonists
    • Many colonists were lower-class citizens
  • All Roman colonies had roads, aqueducts, temples, markets, theaters, baths, Roman-style living accommodations, etc. 

Effects of Colonization
  • Romanization spread without any military expense, cost of life, man-power, or money
  • Homeless people in Rome could be granted a plot of land, some money to start life, and full citizenship status as long as they relocated to a Roman colony.
  • Usually colonies were comprised of people who needed a second chance outside of Rome
    • No one Wanted to live outside the *great* city of Rome, but this was the only way many people could thrive
  • Thousands of Roman families were resettled through colonization 

Southern Italy 
  • Many Greek colonists lived in southern Italy
  • King Pyrrhus was a Greek general and ally of the colonists
    • Pyrrhus wanted to conquer Rome completely
    • He won a series of battles but at such an amazing expense that his name became synonymous with a battle that costs so much that it is not worth fighting - a “Pyrrhic victory” 
  • The Greek colonists realized they could no longer afford Pyrrhus, so they sent him and his elephants on their way
    • This was the first time elephants had been seen in Italy
  • The Greeks came to terms with the Romans
  • By 267 BC, Rome was in total control of the Italian Peninsula 

Carthage and the Punic Wars

Carthage
  • Carthage was a Phoenician colony in North Africa
    • The Latin term for “Phoenician” was “Puni” 
  • Carthage was wealthy
    • The Phoenicians were famous traders of purple dye, which was a luxury good
    • There was also a lot of wheat fields in the area
  • Rome and Carthage interacted as far back as 510 BC, when they signed a peace treaty

The First Punic War
  • After Rome had won control of the Italian peninsula, the Romans looked toward Sicily (dominated by Carthage) 
  • The Romans do not like having a wealthy, foreign power so close to them.
    • Because Carthage was so powerful, the Romans were hesitant to start a war directly
  • The Romans were asked to settle a mercenary dispute in Sicily - while there, they encountered the Carthaginians
  • The Roman Senate decided to war with Carthage
  • Rome needed a navy
    • Romans were not so great at navigation and boating
    • They hired boatmen from Corinth to train men to row
  • The Romans developed a way to fight naval battles “on land” using the Corvus(“crow”)
    • It is a plank that lowers onto another ship, held with a spike. The soldiers cross over and fight the soldiers on the other side. 
    • The other ship stays intact so the Romans can capture and keep it (unlike ramming it)\
  • The Romans won, using their Corvus technique 
  • Carthage settled with Rome
    • Agreed to abandon Sicily completely
    • Paid a 3200 talent fine
  • Rome also take Corsica and Sardinia, which was not part of the treaty
    • This enrages the Barca family (family of Hannibal) 

Effects of the War
  • Rome now has an overseas Province
  • A standing navy is needed with professional sailors
    • Now that Rome has a navy, they can also conquer other places like Egypt or Asia Minor
  • Governers are needed
  • The Roman bureaucracy expands
  • Romans come into contact with Greek drama in Sicily - the Romans particularly loved comedy, which they brought back to Rome
    • Significant because the Romans translated and preserved many Greek plays that otherwise would have been lost

Carthage Recovers
  • Carthage had no intention of becoming a second-rate world power
  • They lost Sicily, Corsica, and Sardinia to Rome, so they decided to take areas in southern Spain 
    • Spain was rich in metal ores needed for weapons
  • The Romans felt threatened, but Carthage argues that they are trying to make enough money to pay off their war debt

Hannibal 
  • Grew up in Carthage; raised to hate Rome
  • Carthaginians practiced child sacrifice to Baal Harmon (“Baal of the Furnace”)
    • Phoenician religion also practiced in Phoenicia 
    • Hundreds of children would be burned at a time
  • Legend states that Hannibal was taken down to the furnace ~5-8 years old and made to put his hand on the altar and swear to hate the Romans
    • Must have been physiologically horrific for him 
  • Trained with his general-father in Spain 
  • After skirmish in Sicily, he planned to take war elephants over the Alps to invade Rome
    • The Gauls had come over the Alps into Italy, so Hannibal knew it was possible 
    • This is a surprise to the Romans, who expected Carthage would attack by way of Sicily
  • Hannibal is kind to towns as he moved toward Rome - he doesn’t force people to join him
    • Most towns stay loyal to Rome because they fear Rome over Hannibal 

The 2nd Punic War
  • Hannibal gets over the Alps with maybe 37 elephants left 
  • He wins some victories against the Gauls in northern Italy, impressing them 
    • So 40,000 troops join his side
  • All the young men of Rome who were hoping to become great heroes (and hoped to be elected consul) claimed they could stop Hannibal
    • One of these men was Fabius Maximus 

Hannibal Cornered
  • Fabius Maximus thought the Romans should let Hannibal burn out on his own
    • Hannibal is having trouble gaining supplies
  • Hannibal becomes cornered with mountains on one side and water on the other due to bad directions from a guide. 
    • Hannibal has the guide crucified
  • The Romans think they will finally beat Hannibal
  • Hannibal has his men gather flammable material, ties it to the horns of cattle, sets it on fire, and the cattle goes crazy, running forward. 

Fabius Maximus 
  • The Romans panicked - they were terrified that the Carthaginians had gone mad
    • They fear fighting insane people (remember the Gauls) 
  • Fabius figured out what happened, but it was too late - Hannibal’s army passed unscathed 
  • Fabius insists that the Romans need to wait Hannibal out, but many young senators disagree
  • The Romans become anxious about Hannibal’s successes and grow weary of Fabius’ delay tactic 
  • Hannibal occupied Italy for ~15 years

Scipio Africanus 
  • Scipio insists that the Senate give him command and is successful
  • He proposes going to Africa to attack Carthage 
  • Fabius convinces the Roman Senate to send Scipio, but Scipio has to raise the money himself
  • As Scipio hoped, Hannibal was called back to Carthage - so he was no longer a threat in Italy 
  • Scipio uses Hannibal’s tactic of a “ring of steel” 
    • Te center of the battle formation steps back, making a “U” shape 
    • The enemy is sucked in, thinking they are retreating
    • The sides fold around the enemy, leaving no escape 

End of 2nd Punic War
  • Scipio also arranges his men in rows so that elephants do not trample them - instead they run past and through them 
  • At the Battle of Zama in 202 BC, Carthage surrenders to Scipio, who earns the epithet “Africanus” -> “the African” as Victor of Africa 
  • As a result, Carthage must: 
    • Evacuate Spain
    • Become a dependent ally of Rome 
    • Pay 10,000 talents (they have 50 years to pay) 
    • Surrender their navy completely

What became of Hannibal 
  • Hannibal returns to Carthage and makes back some of the money lost during the Punic war, but senators blame him for the 2nd Punic War, so he exiled himself. 
  • 2 theories on his death
    • Hannibal’s death occurred after his finger was wounded by his drawn sword while mounting his horse, resulting in a fever and then his death three days later.
    • Sea of Marmara, after having taken poison, which he had long carried about him in a ring
      • ”Let us relieve the Romans from the anxiety they have so long experienced, since they think it tries their patience too much to wait for an old man’s death” 

3rd Punic War (149 BC) 
  • Rome still hates Carthage and wants it destroyed
  • Cato the Elder convinces the Senate that Carthage is still a threat 
    • “Carthage must be destroyed” 
  • The Romans had other reasons to conquer Carthage: 
    • By the middle of the 2nd century BC, the population of the city of Rome was about 400,000 and rising 
    • Feeding the growing populace was becoming a major challenge. 
    • The farmlands of Carthage were the most productive and most easily obtainable (Carthage army is reduced, they will put up less of a fight than part of Greece) 
  • The Carthaginians breached their treaty with Rome when they were attacked by Numidia and defended themselves
    • The treaty stated that Carthage could have no weapons
    • To avoid war they must “Satisfy the Roman People” 
      • Send 300 well bred children
      • turn over all weapons and armor 
    • Rome told Carthage to cease and desist, but Rome would not come to their aid unless Carthage moved their city 10 miles inland, away from their port (while their city burned) 
  • Carthage refuses to move their city 10 miles inland, sparking the 3rd Punic War
  • In 145 BC, Scipio Aemilianus (descendant of Scipio Africanus) destroyed Carthage completely 
    • Scipio Aemilianus sieged Carthage
    • All males over 12 executed on the spot 
    • Women/children sold into slavery (50,000 people) 
    • Everything burned or knocked down (Takes 17 days to burn everything) 
    • Scipio cursed the land 
  • Carthage no longer exists

Taking Greece
  • Two years later, Rome defeats Sparta
  • Rome also destroyed Corinth, which was the best way to take Greece, they take down the Hellenistic world
  • Rome now had control of Carthage
    • They had conquered the west
  • Rome also now had control of Greece
    • They had conquered the east 

Lasting Effects
  • Pax Romana = Roman Peace
  • No longer live a “simple life” of a farmer
  • Some Romans consider the victories a misfortune 
    • beginning of Rome’s troubles
    • Cato the Elder longs for the “Good old days” 
  • Scipio Aemilianus embraced the new way

Patricians and Plebeians: Continued Tension 
  • After the 3rd Punic War, the political and social structure in Rome began to change
    • People coming from the war wanted a place in the government
    • There were many unemployed soldiers
      • Soldiers received plots of land in exchange for service
      • But land was running out in Italy 
      • Many settle far away from the Capitol/Italian Peninsula 
    • Some people were not very good farmers - they came back to Rome and were unemployed when their farms failed
  • A great urban mob was growing in Rome 
  • Rome was profiting from conquest 
    • Building projects: roads, aqueducts, temples
  • All the spoils of war were going to patricians, not plebeians 
    • It was still very rare for plebeians to be elected to public office
  • There was growing discontent as the gap between the rich and poor widened

The Gracchus Brothers: Tiberius and Gaius Gracchus 
  • Born patricians
  • Felt destined to do great work 
  • Mother (Cornelia) had great hopes for them 
    • She was daughter of Scipio Africanus; she wanted to be known for something other than her father
  • Brothers grew up to be reformers 

Tiberius Gracchus 
  • Tiberius was upset by large plantations, “latifundia”, that were worked by gangs of slaves for patrician families - Tiberius was upset by treatment of slaves
  • This was not the Roman “model” of small family farm plots in an ideal agrarian society
  • Tiberius found that many patricians were breaking Roman laws that limited the amount of land a Roman could own
    • Patricians were finding ways around the law and/or intimidating sellers to get more land than they were allowed to have
  • Tiberius decides to champion this cause for the lower classes, through land redistribution
  • He denounced his patrician status to become a plebeian and run for the office of Tribune
    • NEVER done before
  • He proposes a land commission to inventory property and take land from those who legally had too much in order to redistribute it to plebeians
  • Tiberius is looked down on with distant by the Senate (The senate wants life as normal before Tiberius arrived)
  • The King of Pergamum dies and left his wealth to Rome (No heirs) 
    • The Senate decides what to do with the money
    • Tiberius proposes to the people that they use the money to redistribute land that was being held illegally
    • Due to Lex Hortensia, people could do this
    • Senate is outraged, they think their land is up for garbs

The Senate and Tiberius 
  • The Senate claims that Tiberius wants to be king
  • They order the army to kill Tiberius
  • Plebeians all sworn an oath to protect the Tribune, so the army refuses to kill him
  • The consul refuses to go through with this plan (Capital offense to kill a Tribune, Jupiter would be angry) 
  • Senators gather up furniture since they cant carry weapons and they club Tiberius and 300 others to death in the street

Gaius Gracchus 
  • The land commission inventory continues
  • In 123 BC (10 years after Tiberius’ death), Gracchus continued in his brother’s footsteps 
    • Becomes Tribune like his brother and continues his brother’s work
  • He introduces a bill to fix prices on grain
    • The government would set the price of grain at a certain price
    • Before, Sellers just sold at their individual prices
    • Senators loss profit, Plebeians support this bill
  • Gaius wants to extend citizenship to people in Sicily and other nearby areas
  • The bill would also expand citizenship rights for other Roman Citizens: 
    • Right to appeal a magistrate’s sentence
    • Right not to be executed without a trial
  • The Senate tries to make Gaius unpopular
  • The Senate hires a second Tribune (Drusus) to introduce more popular versions of Gaius’ bills (no rent payments anymore, exemptions from flogging for veterans, etc.)
  • Senate now looks like more of a “friend” to plebeians 

The End of Gaius Gracchus
  • The Senate said Gaius is trying to gain power 
    • Gaius’ bodyguard got in a fight with the consul’s servant and killed him
    • The Senate accused Gaius of trying to assassinate the consul 
  • An army forms against Gaius
  • He forms a resistance movement, but he and his followers were killed

Effects: 
  • The government is very different than at the beginning of the Republic
    • Tribunes could be clubbed to death in the streets, patricians choosing to be plebeians, etc. 
  • Civil war, violence, and assassinations became the order of the day
  • The Republic will crumble - this is the beginning of the end
  • The actions of the Senate will set the precedence of using violence will pave the way for Julius Caesar in the next century. 

Roman Republic Wrap Up:

    • Roman Empire has 80 million
    • Majority lived in the East (Greek mainland, Italy)
    • The West is considered (Spain, Africa, Gaul, English Isles) 
    • Romanization spreads on its own, Romans outlawed some things (human sacrifice)

      • Ruling through local city governments

        • Only 150,000 men, Most served then were done

      • Roman army also made infrastructure (Building)
    • Free Trade and Easy Travel
      • Could trade anywhere in the empire (Unified through economy) 
    • Nature of Slavery

      • Number of slaves declines because less enemies to capture and costs too much (free laborours cost much less) 
      • Most people believed that slaves should be freed after 20 years 
    • Medicine and Public Health
      • Average life San is 50 during Roman Empire
        • Romans not subject to pandemic diseases (Bad diseases) until 166 CE 
        • Culture is based off fitness and hygiene 
        • Aqueduct system
        • Mediterranean diet (Heart food) 
    • Pax Romana 
      • First 2 Centuries of Roman Empire are relatively peaceful
      • City states are more peaceful than before conquest
