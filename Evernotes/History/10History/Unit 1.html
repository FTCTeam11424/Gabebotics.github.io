History
  • Change over time
  • story of something
Written/recorded Human history - spans 6000 years
"Truth is relative in history" - truth is relative to who your talking to
Isolation and Snobbery - we learn history to avoid isolation and snobbery
Records
Primary - written/built/drawn by someone in the time period
Secondary - written by someone after the time period about the time period

Historical Principle
  • goal: get as close to the event as possible (primary source trumps secondary source)

Value of secondary sources
  • someone who has studied in great detail - an authority
  • they retell the story of the time in a clear way
Downside of secondary sources
  • they could be wrong
  • could have only focused on one aspect (history of castles not anything else)
  • can be impacted by bias
  • speculation
Human memory - UNRELIABLE
Our job
  • use both sources
  • multiple perspectives

Why study history
  • locates us with our tradition
  • combats temporal isolation and snobbery
  • reveals the perennial elements of human nature
  • promotes a love of humanity and a healthy skepticism
  • without history, you are not an educated human being

How to evaluate the quality of sources
  • contextualizing - building the narrative of the time
  • corroborating - When your sources are in agreement with each other
  • close reading - Absorbing the message while skimming the article

Models of history
  • cyclical - Greek (Thucydides, Plutarch) (Model that thinks history repeats itself)
  • Linear - Jewish and Christian (every event is different, time doesnt repeat itself)
  • Marxist -Aetheistic materiali (history market standpoint)
  • Whig - progress narrative (tend torward enlightenment) (cultural standpoint)
  • Butterfield and Lukacs - developing a historical consiousness
GRAPES - lenses that we look at history through (make sure we look at different perspectives)
G - Geography
R - Religion
A - Art and Architecture
P - Politics and Government
E - Economics
S - Social Structure

Methods of History
Narrative - telling the story(linear/beginning, middle, end)
Argumentation - proving a historical claim, must be situated within the narrative 
Source usage - Argumentation should be proven by primary and secondary sources
Source balance - Sources should reflect multiple perspectives and options, and demonstrate a familiarity with secondary sources.
Source analysis - Sources should be interpreted and explained; just dropping in random quotations is insufficient.

Culture - determine how you act/respond 
Human memory is unreliable (might forget facts, exagerrate things, or completely change) 
Context - description of the setting, background, information relative to the topic
Artifact - raw material, no bias
