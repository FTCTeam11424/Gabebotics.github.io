  • One of the oldest civilizations
  • Geographic Barriers:
    • West and Southwest have high mountain ranges
      • Tien Shan andHimalayas(Impassible)

How Geography Affects Culture: 
  • The two rivers supply basic needs (water, food, irrigation, transportation, trade)
    • Yellow River/Huang He, cradle of Chinese civilization (First area people settled in) (millet (grain), and cattle farming) 
    • Yangtze (longest river in the world, rice)
  • Geography provides folklore, art, poetry, and identity. 
    • Himalayas are considered sacred 
  • Deserts keep most out, except Mongols that live in Gobi Desert

“River of Sorrows” 
  • People first settled in the Yellow River Valley in China 
  • Loess: Yellow silt in the river makes the water that color
  • People feared the rivers - dikes could burst an water flood, crops destroyed, taking lives etc.

Interaction with Other Peoples

Shang China (1600 - 1046 BC) 
  • At 1650 BC, the Shang gained control of part of Northern China, which they held until 1046 BC (Near the Huang He) 
  • Government: 
    • Kings had large palaces and tombs 
    • Noble Class of warriors who helped in battle
    • Princes/Nobles owned most of the land (Noble women could own land)
  • Social Classes: 
    • Royal Family
    • Noble Warriors
    • Artisans/Merchants
    • Peasants (Majority of Society) (Farmers) (Lived in farming villages; difficult lives)
  • Religious Beliefs: 
    • Polytheistic
    • Chief god: Shang DI
      • The king was the link between people and this chief god 
    • The gods heard the prayers of kings and nobles, who were supposed to pray on behalf of all the people.
    • In time, more Chinese began offering prayers to their ancestors for good fortune.
    • Yin and yang - reflected balance of the universe, Yin (earth, darkness, female) and Yang (heaven, light, male) are not against each other, but hold balance. 
  • Writing: 
    • Oracle Bones: (Tortoise Shells) 
      • Priest wrote questions for ancestors and gods to answer
      • Heated until cracked, priests would interpret the cracks
    • Tens of thousands of characters in the Chinese language
    • Writing in calligraphy 
    • Brought unity within China

Zhou Dynasty

Zhou’s Attack on the Shang

  • later his son. King Wu, organized an attack straight into the Shang heartland
This was easy to do, the Shang were vulnerable
  •  King Wu was supported by 8 allied states
  • Led 45,000 troops and 3,000 chariots across the Yellow River and the Mengjin ford, heading north(This costed a LOT of money, marked the beginning of big wars in China)

The Zhou Advantage
  • Although the Shang outnumber the Zhou, the Zhou had superior weapons. (Zhou also had the support of idealists, Shang were blamed for climate and famine)
An improved pole-mounted dagger-axe
Bronze armor
A lot of chariots (3,000)
  • By 1046 BC, the Zhou controlled all Shang territory

Beliefs about Government
  • The Mandate of Heaven - the right to rule
  • This mandate was given to the king, called the “son of heaven”
  • The ruler served as a link between heaven and earth
  • If the ruler was good, gods blessed them, if the ruler was bad, gods punished them
  • Balance/harmony is very important in Chinese religion, if a bad ruler loses balance, then there is suffering 


Zhou Rule
  • Zhou rule brought unprecedented political and cultural unity to this region of China
  • The Zhou considered their subjects a single people, rather than as various ethnic groups.
  • They considered people who were not part of their kingdom less civilized. (Other cultures were not equal, ethnocentric)

Zhou Government
  • The Zhou did not have/want a centralized government
  • They Used a feudal system, with a number of states whose rulers accepted the overall authority of the Zhou kings. (Nobles swore loyalty to king)
  • Similar to feudalism in Europe, Zhou kings enfeoffed nobles, stationing them in strategic garrison towns. (Each town had its own unit, government, etc)
  • Fief: a estate with peasant workers granted to a noble by the king. 

Feudalism 
  • The king would grant fiefs during ceremonies during which oaths were taken
  • The enfoeffed nobility were expected to support the king economically (by sending money) and militarily (by sending soldiers)
  • Kinship system: Zhou sent members of the Royal Family to occupy Shang towns and marry existing powerful lords (who were too strong for Zhou 

Zhou Nobility
  • The Western Zhou kingdom was made up of about 70 feudal states, overseen by lords
  • The nobles lived in cities surrounded by rammed-earth walls (clay walls)
  • Every city had an altar to the god of the soil, a temple of the ancestors, and the ruler’s palace
  • Zhou kings ruled from their capital in the Wei River a Valley, near modern Xian

Farmers 
  • Majority of the population were farmers
  • Farmers lived in semi-subterranean houses
  • They Used two and one-pronged wooden digging sticks for turning the soil
  • Spades were made from bone, shell, or stone attached to wooden handles (metal was too expensive at this time)
  • Used stone, bone, and shell sickles as well
  • Grew millet (staple crop) as well as wheat, rice (in south), beans. 

The Calender
  • Under the Zhou, life revolved around the change of seasons of the agricultural year and the positions of the stars and planet. 
  • A calendar system tracked the progress of the seasons (Calender dated back to the Shang) 
  • Turning point in the year were marked by ritual celebrations (planting, harvest, etc.) 
  • The Calendar continued to develop through the Han Dynasty 

The Importance of Ritual 
  • Rituals of enfeoffment, worship at altars of soil, offering to ancestors, and funeral rituals created a common cultural and political identity across the Zhou Dynasty (Unified the people) (Peasants offer things to agrarian gods, not king/family)
  • Later dynasties would use Zhou rituals to both borrow legitimacy from the ancients and build solidarity and harmony in local communities 
  • The Zhou made important contributions to Chinese philosophy which is borrowed by later dynasties (Even though Zhou falls, their traditions continue)

Correlative Thought 
  • The Zhou saw correlations between the phenomena of the natural world and the emotional, social, and political affairs of humans 
    • These could be both negative or positive: 
      • Negative: A natural disaster is interpreted as a hint that the government of a king is immoral, corrupt, or about to fall. 

View of the Univese
  • The belief that the universe is in a process of constant, but orderly, change
  • People are called to adapt to any current situation by carrying out divination and interpreting texts - this was how to be successful 

Problems in the Zhou Dynasty
  • Legal disputes over land ownership increased
  • Zhou kings were running out of land to grant to new members of the court
  • Zhou weakness was made worse when King Zhao launched a campaign in 957 BC against the Yangtze Valley kingdom of Chu (Chu was getting stronger) 
    • King Zhao lost his own life as well as the main force of the Zhou military 

Reforms
  • King Zhao’s successors undertook reforms to fix increasing problems
  • They began to consider government positions more and considered an official’s talent and performance in addition to hereditary when making officials. 
  • New rituals were introduced (Further unifies community) 

Continuing Problems 
  • Despite efforts toward unity, regionalism developed among the Zhou states 
  • Long distances between the capital and the extensive farmlands that supported the kingdom made transportation difficult
  • Pressure increased from the outside as nomadic peoples threatened the kingdom
  • The nobles’ power increased while the power of the Zhou kings lessened 

The Fall of the Western Zhou 
  • The Western Zhou fell during the reign of King You in 771 BC 
  • In the second year of his reign, the Wei Valley experienced a severe earthquake and eclipses of the sun and moon 
  • Feudal lords were very powerful and profited from peasant labor on their lands
  • The Zhou are forced eastward as feudal lords took possession of Zhou lands
  • After their capital was captured, a weaker Zhou Dynasty was set up in their secondary capital at Luoyang - this dynasty became known as the Eastern Zhou 

Impact of the Western Zhou
  • The rituals of the Zhou as well as their contributions to Chines philosophy continued 
    • The Mandate of Heaven, the idea of the constantly changing universe, and correlated thought
  • The feudal system endured
  • The Zhou kings were used as examples in history texts, both for good and bad
  • Literature from this time period also endured 

The Eastern Zhou 
  • Two major periods in Eastern Zhou history: 
    • Spring and Autumns Period (770-475 BC) 
    • Warring States Period (476-221 BC) 
  • Schools of philosophy flourished (Confucianism and Daoism) 
  • Development of Legalism  

The Multistate System 
  • At the beginning of the Springs and Autumns Period, there were over 120 feudal states in the Eastern Zhou
    • Some were as large as France, others were very small towns
    • Boundaries were not clearly distinguished 
  • It was impossible for the Zhou kings to keep peace between the feudal states 
  • The kings themselves had small amounts of feudal territory with limited resources 

Fighting 
  • The larger states fought smaller states for land 
  • Smaller states made alliances to survive with neighbors or enemies of neighbors 

The Fall of the Zhou
  • The feudal lords became too powerful for the king to control
  • A period of warring between the states occurred for two centuries
  • In 256 BC, a new leader arose from these feudal lords (Zheng, first Qin Emperor) 

Economic Growth
  • Ironworking (China was 2000 years ahead of Europe in metal working) 
    • Stronger weapons, which led to success in battle
    • Axes and Plows
      • Led to increase in food production due to efficiency in plowing/harvesting
  • Money economy (copper coins) made commerce more efficient
  • Economic growth led to population increase 
    • More farmers began settling in the Huang He River valley 

Chinese Achievements
  • Astronomy
    • Calender with 365 (and 1/2) days 
    • Silk 
      • Became China’s most valuable export
      • Silk making secrets were kept for centuries
    • Bookmaking
      • Strips of wood or bamboo were bound together and characters were drawn on the pages

Legalism
  • Legalisms asserted that humans could be dissuaded from acting upon their selfish impulses only if they faced a set of rigidly enforced punishments for evil, selfish behavior (People arent bad, but they can be motivated by selfish behavior, so rules must be enforced to prevent them from acting upon selfishness)
  • Reading and scholarly pursuits were not necessary. Military pursuits are more worthy. 
  • Founded by Hanfeizi (died 233 BC) 
  • Greed motivates actions, which causes conflict
  • Strict laws/harsh punishment is only way to achieve order
  • Ruler alone has power (centralized government) 
  • Impacts: 
    • Effective way to keep order
    • Official policy of the Qin in 221 BC
    • People despised it (Peasants) 

Confucius 
  • Believed in Filial piety (Young respect elders) 
  • Wrote the Analects 
  • Born 551 BC
  • Went from court to court talking to rulers about how to govern 
  • Began teaching
  • His sayings are collected in the Analects (Some believe his disciples wrote the Analects 
  • Developed a philosophy (System of ideas) 
    • Little interest in religious matters
    • About who to keep social order and good government 
  • Confucius emphasizes moral cultivation of individuals, service to the state, and leadership by ethical, educated men
  • Confucius believes there is a universal order, and they must find and maintain it

Confucius Thought 
  • Man is a social being and they must educate themselves they will maintain the moral order and serve the state as moral leaders

Five Relationships 
  • Father - son
  • Elder - younger brother
  • Husband - wife
  • Ruler - subject
  • Friend - friend
  • Only equal relationship is friend - friend 

Responsibilities 
  • Everyone had duties and responsibilities
    • Superiors should care for inferiors and set good example
    • Inferiors owe loyalty and obedience to superiors
    • Women should ensure family stability and promote harmony in home
  • Respect parents above all other duties 
  • Good behavior brings stability
  • Honesty, hard work, and concern for others are most important virtues 

Daoism 
  • Founded by Laois (“Old Master”) 
  • Wrote The Way of Virtue 
  • Not concerned with bringing order to human affairs
  • Sought to live in harmony with nature (Dao = the moral way
  • Looked beyond everyday cares to focus on “the way” of the universe 
  • Rejected conflict/strife
  • Virtue of yielding (Yielding to your environment) 
  • Embraced by Hermits (Reclusive person), Artists, and Poets
  • Government: 
    • Views Govt as unnatural, cause of problems
    • B est govt is the one that governs the least
  • Popular religion: 
    • Daoism evolved into a popular religion
    • Gods, goddesses, magical practices, charms for people, etc.
    • How to act in society and how to view the world

Buddhist Influence 
  • 100 AD - Buddhism spreads to China (Mahayana) (Started in India) 
    • Some conflicts because of value of family life

    • Combination of beliefs

The Qin Dynasty

Government and Expansion
  • The Qin built a series of roads connecting estates
  • They built irrigation systems, which are still in place and used today

The Great Wall
  • Regions to the north were fortified by linking fortifications together, creating a 3,000 mile “Long Wall” 
  • Hundreds of thousands of peasants were sent to work on this project (over 100,000 died) 

Preventing the Empire from Falling
  • Weapons were collected throughout the empire and melted down to create bronze statues (Of himself) 
  • The walls of the capital cities of feudal states were torn down
  • The empire and people were divided into districts 

Censorship 
  • Qin Shi Huangdi regulated strictly what was written 
  • He did not approve of scholars who criticized the government 
  • About 470 scholars were killed to stop the spread of ideas 
    • This may be propaganda by the Han dynasty

Zheng’s Death
  • Qin Shi Huangdi died of “natural causes,” although many think that he poisoned himself on accident in his quest for immortality
    • He supposedly took a pill containing mercury in hope of becoming immortal 
  • He was 49 years old 
    • Total reign was 37 years; he reigned as emperor for 11
  • Bloody civil wars and rebellions followed his death

The Han Dynasty

The Han Dynasty 
  • The Han built on the achievements of the first Qin emperor (Shi Huangdi) to create one of the greatest empires in history, equivalent to the Romans
  • The Han used the same political structure of the Qin and ruled China for the next four centuries (206 BC to 220 AD) 
  • The Han is divided into Western Han and Eastern Han 
    • Western: 206 BC - 9 AD 
    • Eastern: 25 BC - 220 AD
  • During Western Han, the dynasty ruled from the western capital of Changan, located where Xian was. 
  • The dynasty then relocated the capital to the eastern capital of Luoyang.

Lieu Bang (First Han Emperor) 
  • The Han Dynasty began when Liu Bang was victorious over rival rebel forces, after fall of Qin Dynasty
  • Liu Bang’s reign name was Emperor Gaozu pronounced Koso
  • Liu Bang Constructed a new capital at Changan and began the Han Dynasty in 206 BC

Government Organization 
  • Emperor Gaozu used the same administrative structure used by Shi Huangdi 
  • After proclaiming the Han Dynasty, he divided the country into several feudal states to satisfy his wartime allies. 
  • Emperor Gaozu attempted to regain control of much of the land he “gave” away by reincorporating it into the empire in order to not repeat the Warring States Period

After Emperor Gaozu 
  • Emperor Gaozu’s successors introduced a provincial form of governing which divided the country into commanderies which had kingdoms inside of them. 
  • His successors tried to rule this complex state by combining Legalistic and Dalits Methods (There should be laws, but people dont have to be suppressed to be functional in society) 
  • During the reign of Emperor Wudi (140-86 BC) the Han government adopted Confucianism as the official philosophy of the state
