The Polis 
  • Poleis are the city-states of Ancient Greece
  • Many maintained a central meeting and market place (Agora)
  • Each polis maintained its own set of laws and methods for governance
  • Each polis maintained a patron god or goddess, one whom guarded the city. For example, Athens’ patron goddess was Athena. 
  • A majority of city-states were oligarchies
  • Oligarchy: a small group of people having complete a political control of a city-state 
  • oligoi = few, arkhein = to rule, oligoi + arkhein = Oligarchy. 
  • As the poleis were primarily agrarian, land became the most popular resource.
    • This fact, combined with power resting in the hands of a few, led many Greeks to begin looking outward in terms of trade and movement. 

            Colonization
  • This period in Greek history is marked by an increase in trade throughout the Mediterranean
  • This increase in trade led to a fairly rapid colonization of coastal areas, to include Turkey, Spain, Italy, France, and parts of North Africa.

            Tyrants
  • Within the city-states, wealth continued to grow among members outside of the oligarchy, Many of these individuals soon became troubled by the unrestrained power of the oligarchs. Thus, with the help of hoplites, they deposed the oligarchs. 
  • Hoplites: footsoldiers that used a fighting style called a phalanx.
  • Originally, the term tyrant did not have a negative connotation. It simply meant an individual that acquired sole governing power over a society. 
  • As these individuals tended to maintain power by using cruel and oppressive tactics, the term gained a negative meaning

            Draco (650-600 BC) 
  • First lawgiver of Athens
  • Draco provided the first written constitution for Athenians
  • The term “Draconian” developed from these laws. They were unusually harsh in their punishment

            Aristotle
  • Ancient Greek philosopher Aristotle was born circa 284 B.C. In Stagira, Greece.
  • When he turned 17, he enrolled in Plato’s Academy
  • In 338, he began tutoring Alexander the Great 
  • In 335, Aristotle founded his own school, the Lyceum, in Athens, where he spent most of the rest of his life studying, teaching, and writing.
  • One of the main focuses of Aristotle’s philosophy was his systematic concept of logic
  • Aristotle’s objective was to come up with a universal process of reasoning that would allow man to learn every conceivable thing about reality. 
  • The initial process involved describing objects based on their characteristics, states of being and actions.
  • Aristotle defied the main components of reasoning in terms of inclusive and exclusive relationships.  

            Aristotle’s Works
  • Aristotle’s written work also discussed the topics of matter and form. 
  • In his book Metaphysics, he clarified the distinction between the two
  • Nicomachean Ethics and Eudemian Ethics are Aristotle’s major treatises on the behavior and judgment that constitute “good living” 
  • In politics, Aristotle examined human behavior in the context of society and government 

        Aristotle(cont’d) 
  • Aristotle is the father of modern political science
  • Political science - the branch of knowledge that deals with systems of government; the analysis of political activity and behavior. 


            Aristotle - The Politics
  • The modern word ‘political’ derives from the Greek politikos ‘of, or pertaining to, the polis’. (The greek term polis will be translated here as ‘city-state’)
  • Political science studies the tasks of the politician or statesman (politikos), in much the way that medical science concerns the work of the physician. 
  • Aristotle defines the existence of the city-state in terms of the four causes. 
  • 1) Constitution - the constitution is not a written document, but an immanent organizing principle, analogous to the soul of an organism.
  • 2) A ruler - a community of any sort can possess order only if it has a ruling element or authority. 
  • 3) There must be a lawgiver
  • 4) The community must aim at some good
