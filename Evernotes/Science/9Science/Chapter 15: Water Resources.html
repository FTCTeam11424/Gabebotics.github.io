Lesson 15.1 Earth: The Water Planet 

            Where Is Our Water? 
  • Earth’s water comes from the ocean, groundwater, and ice. 
  • Fresh water is both a renewable resource and a limited resource.
  • Earth’s fresh water is distributed unequally. How much water people use depends on where they live and the time of year. 
  • A small amount of the total water on earth is fresh water. 

            Surface Water
  • Surface water includes still bodies of water and river systems. 
  • Watersheds include all of the land area that supplies water to a river system. 
  • Every waterway defines a watershed. 

            Groundwater
  • Groundwater seeps through the soil and becomes contained in underground aquifers.
  • Aquifers are permeable layers of rock and soil that hold water.
  • The water table seperates the zone of saturation from the zone of aeration. 
  • Zone of saturation: In between the permeable and impermeable layers. Area of soil where rock and soil is completely covered in water. Saturated area. 
  • Zone of aeration: Has some moist particles but not many water particles. Not Saturated.
  • The Water Table: The area where the zone of saturation and the zone of aeration meet. 
  • Dry well: A well where the zone of saturation drops below the well’s reach. 
  • Spring: Area where the water table meets the surface, and ground water naturally trickles to the surface. 
  • Recharge zone: Low lieing area where water collects, causing water to soak into the ground. 
  • Aquifers release 1.9 trillion L (492 billion gal) of ground water to the surface each day via springs, geysers, and wells. 
  • We cannot drink from geysers because it is heated and the minerals are dissolved into the water.
  • We can drink from wells because it is cool and the minerals are not dissolved into the water. 
  • Artesian wells: A spring with a well built around it. 

Lesson 15.2 Uses of Fresh Water

            How We Use Water
  • Three main uses of fresh water:
    • Agricultural: Watering crops
    • Industrial: Cooling down equipment. 
    • Personal: Washing, Brushing teeth, Drinking, Cooking

            Using Surface Water
  • Most freshwater used in the U.S. is surface water.
  • Surface water is diverted by canals and dams
  • Drought and overuse have caused significant surface water depletion.

            Using Groundwater
  • 68% of groundwater in the U.S. is used for irrigation, which is very inefficient. (Because giant sprinking system is spraying water across the garden. Some water is wasted using this system. Some water goes into the atmosphere/gets evaporated) 
  • Groundwater mining turns groundwater into a nonrenewable resource because it is withdrawn from the ground faster than it can be replaced. 
  • When groundwater is depleted, the falling water tables can cause cities to sink, and undrinkable saltwater to move into the depleted aquifers. (Water gone, more space, ground sinks into the former saturated zone) 

            Solutions to Freshwater Depletion
  •   Increase supply: 
    • Desalination: “Making” fresh water by removing salt from saltwater 
      • Desalination: Salt Water is boiled into water vapor. Water is then cooled and condenses into fresh water. Fresh water is then collected. 
  • Decrease Demand:
    • Agriculture: Drip-irrigation, climate-appropriate plants
    • Industrial: Water-conserving processes, recycling wastewater to cool machinery.
    • Personal: Xeriscaping(Form of landscaping that preserves water. Basically planting climate appropriate plants or using droughts tolerate plants. JUST THE PRECIPITATION KEEPS THE PLANT ALIVE), water conservation. 

Lesson 15.3 Water Pollution

            Types of Water Pollution
  • Point-source pollution: From a discrete location, like a factory or sewer pipe. 
  • Nonpoint-source pollution: From many places spread over a large area, such as when snowmelt runoff picks up pollutants along its path. 

            Nutrient Pollution
  • Excess phosphorous and other nutrients in the water is nutrient pollution.
  • Eutrophication occurs naturally.
  • Nutrient Pollution can cause cultural eutrophication.
  • Eutrophication: Excess nutrients that cause pollution

           Toxic Chemical Pollution
  • Occurs when harmful chemicals are released into waterways
  • Can be organic or inorganic
  • Harms ecosystems and causes human health problems 
  • Examples: Oil, Toxic Waste 

            Sediment and Thermal Pollution
  • Sediment Pollution: 
    • Unusually large amounts of sediment that can change an aquatic environment.
    • Sediment pollution results from erosion
    • Can degrade water quality, cause photosynthesis rates to decline, and disrupts food webs.
    • Can decrease the photic zone
  • Thermal Pollution: 
    • A heat source that raises the temperature of a waterway
    • Heated water holds less oxygen
    • Examples: Factories pumping in lake water to heat machinery, then pumping it out. 

            Biological Pollution
  • Biological pollution occurs when pathogens enter a waterway.
  • Biological pollution causes more human health problems than any other form of water pollution.
  • Water treatment reduces biological pollution. 
  • Examples: Cholera and E-Coli 

            Groundwater Pollution
  • Sources of groundwater pollution include natural sources, surface pollutants leaching through soil, and leaky underground structures. 
  • Chemicals break down more slowly ing roundwater than in surface water.
  • Most efforts to reduce groundwater pollution focus on prevention. 
  • Examples: Salt, Coal, etc. are natural ways that groundwater is polluted. 

            Ocean Water Pollution
  • Oil pollution in the ocean comes from many widely spread small sources. Natural seeps are the largest single source. Organisms are able to eat the oil however, keeping the ocean clean. 
  • Ocean organisms bioaccumulate mercury pollution (Fish get mercury from their environment and eating other fish, larger fish have more mercury)
  • Nutrient pollution can cause red tides. (Red tides are algae blooms that cause our nervous system to be disrupted. This can be deadly).

            Controlling Water Pollution
  • Government regulation decreases water pollution
  • The Clean Water Act 
    • Set water pollution standards.
    • Required permits to release point-source pollution.
    • Funded sewage treatment plant construction. 

            Water Treatment
  • Drinking water is treated to remove pollutants before humans consume it. 
  • Wastewater is treated to remove pollutants before human-used water is released back to the environment. 
  • Ceptic tanks are used to treat water. 
  • Drainage field is where the clean water goes after being treated.
