            Mineral Formation 
Minerals can form in four ways: 
  • crystallization from magma or lava
  • Precipitation
  • Pressure and temperature(occurs underground)
  • Production by organisms

            Mineral Classes
  • Minerals are classified into groups based on what they are made of

            Rocks
  • Naturally occurring solids made up of minerals and mineral-like materials (Something that is organic, but not considered a mineral)
  • Three types: igneous, sedimentary, metamorphic
  • The rock cycle slowly changes rocks from one type to another through heating, melting, cooling, weathering, and erosion.

            Types of Rocks
  • Igneous: Forms when magma cools and solidifies: Can be intrusive or extrusive (under ground or above ground)
  • Sedimentary: Forms when sediments cement together or when water evaporates and leaves behind minerals; can be clastic(left behind through evaporation), chemical(using a chemical that sticks rocks together), or biochemical(created by a living organism).
  • Metamorphic: Form when heat or pressure changes the crystalline structure of existing rock.

Lesson 11.2 Mining

            What is mined?
  • Ores: Groups of minerals that are mined so metal or metals can be removed (must be a useful metal mixed in with rocks)
  • Nonmetallic minerals: minerals, including gemstones, that as a whole have valuable properties (no metal, usually gemstones, such as diamonds, emeralds, rubies, quartz, salt, granite, etc.)
  • Fuels: Minerals that can be used to generate energy (coal, etc.)

            Process of mining and mineral use 
  • You must first mine minerals
  • You must then remove the minerals from the ground
  • Then they must be refined and processed
  • Some things require multiple processings while others do not

            Mining Methods
  • Strip Mining: Removing strips of ground in order to get to a shallow deposit
  • Subsurface mining: digging deep under ground to get resources. Shafts/tunnels.
  • Open pit mining: Blow a giant hole in the ground and dig in that hole. Shallow ISH deposits. Between strip and subsurface levels.
  • Mountaintop removal: Blowing up the top of a mountain and then mining(how we get things like granite)
  • Solution Mining: Build a tunnel down to deposit. Add hot water, which dissolves materials. You can then suck up the solution and evaporate the water to get the resource. Requires a material that dissolves easily.
  • Placer Mining: Using a pan to sift through sand/dirt to get to minerals. Using water to dissolve the sand/dirt
  • Undersea Mining: Mining under the sea, used for oil. 

            Processing Ore
  • Ores require processing
  • First: Crusing and grinding the material (typically happens on the site of the mine)
  • Two: Froth Flotation:  Make a slushy out of the material, then add a chemical that makes it float. Metal floats, rock sinks. This makes the metal HYDROPHOBIC Breaking down the ore into metal and tailings. (Tailings is wasteful rock we dont need).
  • Three: Smelting down the metal to concentrate it down into specific chunks. 
  • Most metals need a fourth step: Processing to make it useful,  Example: from iron to stainless steel., adding carbon to iron.

Lesson 11.3 Mining Impacts and Regulation

            Environmental Impacts of Mining
  • Increased erosion
  • Sediments and debris clog waterways
  • Acid drainage and other forms of water pollution
  • Air pollution
  • Disruption of ocean ecosystems (undersea mining)

            Social Impacts of Mining
  • Property Damage caused by mountaintop removal or mine collapse (property value goes down as well, making it harder to sell) 
  • Although mining can bring money and jobs to poor areas of the world, conflicts can arise over mineral rights. (People argue about who owns the minerals that are mined)
  • Mining can be hazardous to the health of the miners. (Many miners cannot afford healthcare)

            General Mining Law of 1872
  • Created rules to manage mining activity, but also promoted mining.
  • Public land can be claimed and leased from the government for mining.
  • Claim owners can file to patent, or own, the land for $5 per acre. 
  • Amendments are currently being considered.

            Additional Mining Regulations
  • Mineral Leasing Act of 1920: governs leasing of public lands for fossil fuel, phosphate, sodium, and sulfur mining.
  • Surface Mining Control and Reclamation Act (1977): requires that coal-mining companies restore land after mining is complete.

            Mining Safety
  • First law, passed in 1891, established ventilation requirements for coal mines and prohibited miners under 12.
  • Today the Federal Mine Safety Health Act of 1977 regulates mine safety.
  • In 2010, an underground explosion in a Waste Virginia mine killed 29 miners.

            Responsible Mineral Use
  • Minerals are nonrenewable resources
  • Reducing use, reusing, and recycling minerals can help minimize the negative impacts of mining and address limited supplies.
