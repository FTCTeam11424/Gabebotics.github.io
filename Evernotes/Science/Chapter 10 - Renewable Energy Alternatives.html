Lesson 10.1 Renewable Resources

        Why we need alternatives
  • Economic reasons: 
    • fossil fuels wont last forever
    • renewable resources provide new jobs 
    • Our country will be less dependant on others for fuel
  • Environmental reasons: 
    • Renewable resources will decrease air pollution and green house gas emissions.

                Biomass
    • Biomass: a material from living or once living organisms
    • Biomass Energy: Energy produced through burning biomes. The fuel that biomass energy creates is called biofuel

                Biomass Energy
    • Solids: wood, charcoal, manure, agricultural and timber waste, switchgrass
    • Liquids such as Ethanol can be considered biofuel
    • Benefits: 
      • No net change in atmospheric carbon dioxide
      • Can be produced by all nations
    • Costs: 
      • Takes away land from food crops or natural habitats 
      • Deforestation, soil erosion, and desertification can result if plant biomass is harvested too rapidly. 
      • Large energy input is needed (not energy efficient)
      • Burning biomass indoors can lead to indoor air pollution. 

                Geothermal Energy
    • Underground heat generated by high pressures and breakdown of radioactive elements.
    • Usually accessed by drilling deep below the ground; steam turns turbines generating electricity.
    • Can be used directly by piping hot water from its source into homes and businesses. 
    • Benefits: 
      • Causes much less air pollution than fossil fuels
      • Low green house emissions costs
    • Costs: 
      • Not sustainable if ground water is used faster than it is naturally replaced
      • Hot groundwater can contain pollutants that damages machines or add to pollution
      • Some geothermal energy projects can trigger earthquakes
      • Geothermal power plants can only be built in places with easy access to geothermal energy. 

    Lesson 10.2 Hydropower and Ocean Power

                Generating Electricity With Hydropower
    • Hydropower is generated by turbines turned by moving water.
    • Two basic approaches:
      • Water flows through a dam and pushes turbines.
      • Naturally flowing water is diverted through turbines.
    • Naturally flowing water can lead to a variable supply of electricity. 
    • However, if there is a slow moving river, you can make dams.
    • Dams:
      • Dams provide constant electricity but can disturb natural habitats.
      • Dams are for slow flowing rivers. It creates higher water pressure for one side causing the flow to increase, generating more energy.

                Benefits and Cost of Hydrpower
    Benefits:
    • Completely Renewable
    • No air pollution or greenhouse gas emissions
    • Yields relatively cheap electricity
    Costs:
    •  Dams alter ecosystems by flooding and affect organisms (especially fish because it alters migration patterns) (can be fixed by adding a door on the bottom of the damn to let fish through)
    • Dams trap soil-enriching silt, preventing it from reaching downstream. (Can be fixed by picking up soil and placing it on the other side of the damn)
    • Building dams and reservoirs can displace people by flooding. 

                Tidal Energy
    • Tides is the fluctuation of water in the ocean.
    • Tidal energy is the energy generated by the flow of ocean water as tides go in and out.
    • Tidal waters push turbines in a dam (must be a lot of tidal difference)
    • The best place to harness tidal energy have big differences in the heights of high and low tides.
    • Generates little to no pollution, but shore ecosystems can be negatively affected and very few location are currently suitable.

                Thermal Energy From the Ocean
    • The ocean abosrbs slar energy and stores it as heat.
    • Ocean thermal energy conversion (OTEC) converts heat energy in the ocean to electricity.
    • Warm ocean water is used to boil liquids with very low boiling points, generating vapors that can spin turbines (Cannot be water)
    • OTEC technology is currently under development; no power plants use this method today. (Hard to find a liquid with low enough boiling point, Hard to make a facility, hard to transport energy, not feasable at the moment) 

    Lesson 10.3 Solar and Wind Energy

                Harnessing Solar Energy For Heat
    • Passive solar heating: Designing a building to efficiently capture, store, and distribute the sun’s energy; can be used to heat homes and businesses (example: greenhouse)
    • Active solar heating: Uses technology such as solar panels to capture, store, and distribute the sun’s energy. (Example: solar panels)
        
                Harnessing Solar Energy to make Electricity
    • Photovoltaic cells (solar panels): Convert solar energy directly into electricity
    • Concentrating solar power: uses mirrors to focus the sun’s rays on a vessel containing fluid; creates steam to push turbines and generate electricity.

                Benefits and Costs of Solar Power
    Benefits: 
    • Inexhaustible
    • Clean-no air or water pollution produced during operation
    • Low maintenance devices
    • New jobs to make solar devices
    Costs:
    • Some pollution during manufacture (very large trucks to build solar panels, and a factory for it)
    • Many regions aren’t sunny enough.
    • Devices are expensive.

                Using Wind To Make Electricity
    • Wind turbines (windmills) convert wind’s kinetic energy to electrical energy
    • Wind turbines can be placed on land or offshore
    • Turbines can be solitary or built in groups called wind farms.
                
                Benefits and Costs of Wind Power
    Benefits: 
    • No pollution or greenhouse gases produced during operation
    • Under good conditions, produces far more energy than it uses
    • Relatively cheap to operate
    Costs: 
    • High startup costs
    • Winds can be unpredictable
    • Fastest winds are often not near population centers
    • Communities complain about the looks and noise of wind farms
    • Can be harmful to birds and bats

    Lesson 10.4 Energy From Hydrogen

                Hydrogen As A Fuel Source
    • Hydrogen gas can be combusted to produce heat, with just water as a byproduct
    • On earth, hydrogen is commonly found in compounds such as water, though it is rare in its elemental form, Hydrogen Gas(Rockets use most of our hydrogen gas. Can be obtained by breaking down water and methane).
    • Hydrogen can be produced by breaking down water, or methane.
    • A new technology involves using green algae to produce Hydrogen gas from water.

                Benefits and Costs of Hydrogen as a Fuel Source
    Benefits: 
    • Inexhaustible supply of hydrogen
    • Few greenhouse gases or pollutants
    • Water and heat might be the only byproducts
    • Can be stored and transported
    Costs:
    • Hydrolysis(Splitting of water to generate hydrogen gas) is expensive, and breaking down methane yields carbon dioxide. Both require energy from an outside source.
    • Hydrogen gas must be compressed if used for vehicle fuel.

                Fuel Cells
    • Hydrogen and Oxygen react to form water, producing electricity in the fuel cell’s electrodes
    • Only byproducts are water vapor and heat
    • Can power vehicles or power plants
    • Can provide electricity to places “off grid” or unreachable by conventional power companies.
