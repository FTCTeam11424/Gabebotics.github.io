Chemistry of Life
  • Matter: anything that has mass and occupies space 
  • Mass: amount of matter in an object
  • Weight: Measure of the pull of gravity on an object
  • All matter is composed of atoms 
  • Element: A substance made up of one type of atom
    • Atom is the smallest part of an element that has its properties
Atomic Structure
  • Each atm is made up of smaller parts called protons, electrons, and neutrons
  • Protons (+) in nucleus
  • Neutrons (0) in nucleus 
  • Electrons (-) in a cloud outside 

There are SIX elements necessary for life
  • C - Carbon
  • N - Nitrogen 
  • P - Phosphorous 
  • H - Hydrogen
  • O - Oxygen
  • S - Sulfur 

Periodic Table
  • Elemental Symbol
    • Unique one/two letter symbol
  • Atomic # of a neutral element
    • Equals the # of protons and the # of electrons
    • Unique for each element
  • Atomic Mass #
    • Average mass of an atom of the element
    • Generally equals the SUM of protons and neutrons 
  • Protons + Neutrons = Mass #
  • Mass # - Atomic # = Neutrons 

The Nucleus
  • Middle of the atom (central core
  • Holds positive charged protons and neutral neutrons 
  • Overall has a positive charge
  • Contains most of the mass of the atom

The Protons
  • All atoms of a given element have the same number of protons 
  • # of protons = Atomic # 
  • # of protons = # of electrons (in a neutral atom) 

The Neutrons 
  • The number varies slightly among atoms of the same element
  • Different number of neutrons produce isotopes of the same element 
  • Neutrons change mass but not the charge of the atom

Isotopes 
  • An element with the same # of protons, but different # of neutrons 

The Electrons 
  • Negatively charged high energy particles with little or no mass
  • Travel at very high speeds in different energy levels 
    • Energy levels are different distances from the nucleus 

Electron Cloud
  • Made of shells: 
    • inner most -> can hold at most 2 electrons 
    • Next most -> can hold at most 8 electrons 
    • Third outer most -> can hold at most 8 electrons 
  • Electrons want to have full shells, because this makes them stable!
  • The electrons on the most outer shell are called VALENCE electrons 
  • Example: Carbon (neutral) 
    • Atomic # = 6 -> 6 total electrons 
      • 2 electrons fill the inner most shell 
      • 4 electrons in the outer most shell
    • Carbon has 4 valence electrons 
    • Can bond to 4 other atoms

Periodic Table 
  • Elements are arranged by their atomic number on the Periodic Table
  • The horizontal rows are called Periods and tell the number of energy levels 
  • Vertical groups are called families and tell the number of valence electrons 

Molecules and Compounds 
  • Molecules - 2+ atoms covalently bonded together
    • Ex. O2 for Oxygen
  • Compound - 2+ elements bonded together
    • H20 for water and NaCl for sodium chloride

Compounds 
  • Compounds have different properties than individual elements 
    • Ex. NaCl = 1 sodium + 1 chlorine -> table salt 
  • What happens when the ratio of atoms changes?
    • When the ratio of elements in a compound changes, the physical and chemical properties are also changed

Molecules
  • A Molecule is two or more atoms held together by covalent bonds 
    • Almost all of the substances that make up organisms, from lipids to nuclei acids to water, and molecules are held together by covalent bonds
    • A molecule is a type of compound. All molecules are compounds, but all compounds are NOT molecules 
      • Molecule Ex. H20, 02, 03
      • Compound: NaCl, H20

Bonds
  • Molecular “glue” 
  • Holds atoms together to form compounds
  • Valence electrons are involved in bonding
  • Atoms are most stable when their outer most energy level is filled

Bonding 
  • What holds atoms together? 
    • Atoms are held together by chemical bonds
  • Goal: To fill outer electron shell. 
  • Atoms bond together (Share/take/give away) to get a full outer shell

Covalent Bonds
  • Formed when two atoms SHARE one or more pairs of electrons. 
  • Called molecules
  • Between two nonmetals

Ionic Bonds
  • Formed when one or more electrons are transferred from metal to nonmetal 
  • Atom loses or gains electrons 
    • If atom loses electrons -> becomes positively charged 
    • If atom gains electrons -> becomes negatively charged 
  • Atoms that have gained or lost electrons are ions. 

Cations & Anions
  • Cation: when an atom loses an electron, it becomes more positively charged
  • Anion: When an atom gains an electron, it becomes more negatively charged

States of Matter
  • Atoms are in constant motion (called kinetic energy) 
  • The rate at which atoms or molecules in a substance move determines its state

Levels of Organization 
  • Subatomic particles
  • Atom (O)
  • Element (O)
  • Molecule (O2)
  • Macromolecules (Lipid)
  • Cell (Life begins here) 
  • Tissue (epithelial tissue 
  • Organ (lung)
  • Organ system (Respiratory system)
  • Organism (Human)

Water
  • Water: 1 atom of oxygen and 2 atoms of hydrogen held together by covalent bonds (H20) 
  • Water is a Polar Molecule -> unequal distribution of charges
    • One side is more positive
    • One side is more negative
    • Dissolves in water 
      • Ex. Water


Polar and Nonpolar
  • Polar Molecule -> unequal distribution of charges
    • One side is more positive
    • One side is more negative
    • Dissolves in water 
      • Ex. Water
  • Nonpolar Molecule: no separation of charge, so no positive or negative poles are formed 
    • Do not dissolve in water
    • Ex. CO2, 02, lipids

Interactions of Water Molecules
  • Polar water molecules can be attracted to each other
  • The hydrogen atom with its partial positive charge is attracted to the oxygen atom with its partial negative charge

Hydrogen Bonds
  • Bonding between polar molecules
  • Very weak, but very important for the characteristics of water
  • Covalent bonds internally hold a water molecule together
    • Different water molecules are held together externally by very weak hydrogen bonds 

Five Qualities of Water

Cohesion
  • The attraction between molecules of the same substance (water)
  • Tendency of molecules of the SAME “kind” to stick together
  • Causes HIGH surface tension (water bugs)
    • Measure of how difficult it is to stretch or break the surface of a liquid/resist an external force

Adhesion 
  • The attraction between two different molecules
    • Glass, soil, plant tissue, cotton, etc.
  • Forms stronger bonds than cohesion
    • Ex. Meniscus forming on graduated cylinder
    • Ex. Capillary action - seen in plants, trees, and with straws too!

High Specific Heat
  • The temperature of water does not increase or decrease easily
    • Water has to absorb more heat energy to increase overall temperature compared to other compounds
  • Helps to regular cell temperature in organisms
    • Its temperature does NOT fluctuate very much -> allows life to live in water
  • Moderate Earth’s climate by buffering large fluctuations in temperature(Balloon without water pop, balloon with water not pop)

Low Density in Solid Form
  • Solid water (ice) is less dense than liquid water
    • Therefore it floats in liquid water
  • Since ice floats in water -> Life can exist under frozen surfaces of lakes/polar seas

Water as a Solvent
  • What is a solution? 
    • A solution is a mixture of substances that looks the same throughout 
      • Made up of a solute and a solvent
  • Water is the universal solvent because it is so good at it!
  • Solute - What gets dissolved (Lemonade power)
  • Solvent - Does the dissolving (Water)
  • Solution - Uniform mixture of two or more substances (Lemonade)

Hydrophilic and Hydrophobic 
  • A hydrophilic substance
    • has an affinity for water
    • Water “loving” 
    • Dissolves in water
      • Example: Salt and Sugar
  • A hydrophobic substance
    • Does not have an affinity for water
    • Water “fearing” 
    • Does NOT dissolve in water
      • Example: Oil
