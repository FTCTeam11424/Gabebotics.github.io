Lesson 14.1 Defining Biomes

            Earth’s Major Biomes
  • Groups of terrestrial ecosystems that share biotic and abiotic conditions
  • 10 Primary Biomes: 
    • Tropical rain forest
    • Dry forest 
    • Savanna
    • Desert
    • Temperate rain forest
    • Temperate forest
    • Temperate grassland
    • Chaparral
    • Booreal forest
    • Tundra

            Climate and Climatographs
  • Climate: Average conditions, including temperature and precipitation, over long periods of time in a given area
  • Weather: Day-to-day conditions in Earths atmosphere
  • Climatographs: Diagrams that summarize an area’s average monthly temperature and precipitation(graph that tells you climate)
  • Each biome has a set of characteristic organisms adapted to its particular climate conditions.

            Biomes and Net Primary Production
  • Gross: (All of the money you earned)
  • Net: (Amount of usable money you take home)
  • Gross primary production: The rate at which primary producers undergo photosynthesis(All of the energy absorbed through plants).  
  • Net primary production: The amount of organic matter (biomass) that remains after primary producers use some to carry out cellular respiration(Any energy usable by other organisms).
  • Ecosystems vary in their net primary productivity, the rate at which primary producers convert energy to biomass.
  • Warm, wet biomes generally have higher net primary productivity than cold, dry biomes.

Lesson 14.2 Biomes

            Tropical Rain Forest 
  • Year-round warm temperatures and at least 2 m (6.6 ft) precipitation a year
  • Soil generally nutrient-poor(Plants use all of the nutrients) 
  • Forest canopy, emergent layer, and understory support enormous variety of plants
  • Canopy trees have a ton of tiny trees and grow very fast to compete for sunlight. Shallow but large roots.
  • Understory trees have shallow roots, and very large leaves to get as much sunlight as possible.
  • Has highest Net productivity.
  • Plants tend to have large, flat leaves and shallow roots.
  • Supports more animal species than any other biome; animals tend to be highly specialized(Animals must be able to climb trees, Birds are small so that they can maneuver through forest) 
Epiphytes: plants that grow on other trees to absorb sunlights. Does not harm the tree it is on. 

            Tropical Dry Forest
  • Warm year-round, but rainfull highly seasonal. (Doesnt rain for half of the year, rains a lot in the other half)
  • Most trees are deciduous (they lose their leaves and cease photosynthesis part of the year)(They lose their leaves in the dry season).
  • Plants and animals exhibit adaptations (e.g. waxy leaf coating(Slows down transpiration), deep roats(Most water possible), estivation(hybernating but in the dry season), migration) that enable them to survive the dry season.

            Savanna 
  • Receives less precipitation than tropical dry forests, but more than deserts; usually has a distinct rainy season
  • Grasses interspersed with groups of trees
  • Tree growth limited by frequent fires and strong winds
  • Plants are adapted to dry conditions; tend to be deciduous with deep roots, thick bark(Protecting heart of the tree so that it can continue to grow), and waxy coatings on leaves. 
  • Many animals migrate to find water, or burrow when water is scarce. (No point in estivation.)

            Desert
  • Receives less than 25 cm (9.8 in) of precipitation per year
  • Temperatures vary widely from day to night.
  • Plants tend to have thick, leathery leaves, store water in their tissues, and have shallow roots.(The cactus have spike leaves. The spikes are leaves. The Cactus adapted to have photosynthesis in the stem. 
  • Animals get most of their water from the food they eat, and they tend to be nocturnal. Mammals have exaggerated appendages(To spread out the heat more to help lower body temperature.) to help regulate body temperature.

            Temperate Rain Forest
  • Year-round moderate temperatures and heavy rainfall.
  • Largest extent found in Pacific Northwest of United States
  • Characterized by tall evergreen trees, such as cedars and hemlocks, that dont lose leaves annually; many are conifers (produce seed-bearing cones)
  • Forest floor is shaded, damp, covered in moss.
  • Animals that require moisture, such as amphibians, thrive here.

            Temperate Forest
  • Precipitation evenly spread throughout the year
  • Varied temperatures (hot summers, cold winters)
  • Plants tend to be broad-leafed(collects more sunlight) and deciduous
  • Soil is enriched with nutrients from annual leaf drop.(Soil is enriched because there is lots of dead animals to decompose and enrich the soil)
  • Animals may migrate, hibernate, or store food to survive cold conditions.
  • WE live in a Temperate Forest

            Temperate Grassland (Prairie)
  • Moderate seasonal precipitation and fairly extreme seasonal temperatures; droughts and fires common
  • Not enough precipitation to suport large trees; grasses, which grow from their base, thrive despite droughts, fires, and animals grazing
  • Trees collect nutrients from leaves that are above ground, while grasses collect nutrients from roots that are under ground. This makes grasses more suitable for the droughts.
  • Animals are adapted to deal with lack of cover( Ex: Thick coats and burrowing)
  • Soil tends to be rich in nutrientsl most of world’s grasslands have been converted to farmland.

            Chaparral
  • Highly seasonal conditions with mild, wet winters and warm, dry summers.
  • Prolonged hot, dry periods: droughts and fires common
  • Plants are drought-resistant; many have thick, waxy leaves or leaves with hairs that trap moisture; succulents are common.
  • Plants may have thick bark and deep roots to resist fire; some plants require fire to germinate.(They use fire to get rid of competition)
  • Many animals burrow or are nocturnal to avoid heat.

            Boreal Forest(Taiga)
  • Long, cold winters; short, cool summers
  • Nutrient-poor, slightly acidic soils (Coldness causes soil to be acidic)
  • Low species diversity
  • Coniferous trees with waxy needles and conical shape, adapted to harsh, snowy conditions are common. (Large leaves carry a lot of snow and break off) 
  • Animals feed, breed, and care for young mostly during short warm seasonl year-round residents tend to have thick insulation and small extremities that maintain heat.(Animals tend to have a short gestation and infant/adolescent phases. 

            Tundra
  • Extremely cold, dark winters; relatively sunny and cool summers
  • Found at very high latitudes in the Northern Hemisphere
  • Harsh winds, nutrient-poor soil, and freezing temperatures limit plant growth; no tall trees; mosses and lichens common
  • Characterized by permafrost(underground soil that is frozen year-round)
  • Birds and caribou migrate to the tundra during the mild summer to feed on insects and lichens, which are one of the few species that are able to live here all year. 

            Polar Ice and Mountains
  • Not classified as biomes
  • No land under polar ice in Northern Hemisphere; ice sits atop Antarctica in Southern Hemisphere.
  • Very few plants; most life is in surrounding ocean
  • Mountain communities change with elevation, similar to how biome communities change with latitude.

Lesson 14.3 Aquatic Ecosystems

            Describing Aquatic Ecosystems
  • Salinity: the amount of dissolved salt present in water. Ecosystems are classified as salt water(Anything greater than 30 ppt), fresh water(Anything less than 7 parts per thousand), or brackish(between 7 and 30 ppt) depending on salinity 
  • Photosynthesis tends to be limited by light availability, which is a function of depth and water clarity.
  • Aquatic ecosystems are either flowing or standing.(flowing is moving water, standing is not moving water)
  • Aquatic Ecyosystem zones: Photic, aphotic, benthic.
    • Photic: Surface area, however deep sunlight reaches in the water (Photosynthesis can only happen in the Photic zone)
    • Aphotic: Non-Sunlight section of the ocean, all areas that are not lit by the sun
    • Benthic: Ground on ocean floor (Part of the benthic zone is also in the Photic zone and part is in the Aphotic zone.)

            Freshwater Ecosystems: Ponds, Lakes, Inland Seas
  • Salinity is less than 0.5 ppt (parts per thousand)
  • Ponds and lakes are similar, except in size but inland seas
  • litteral zone: The area of Benthic zone that is in the Photic zone. Plants can grow in the litteral zone.
  • Limnetic zone: The area of Benthic zone that is in the Aphotic zone.
   
            Freshwater Ecosystems: Wetlands
  • Areas of land flooded with water at least part of the year
  • Include freshwater marshes, swamps, bogs, and fens.
  • Wetlands prevent flooding(They soak up water), recharge aquifers(Ground water sources), filter pollutants(), and provide habitats(Snakes, salamanders, alligators, fish).

            Freshwater Ecosystems: Rivers and Streams
  • Bodies of surface water that flow downhill, eventually reaching an ocean or inland sea.
  • Watershed: The area of land drained by a river and its tributaries. Anything that fills into the river. 
  • Characteristics, such as dissolved oxygen(Faster flow, more oxygen is pumped in), temperature, water speed, organisms, and others, change from source to mouth.
  • Tributary: Small rivers that feeds to a main or big river.
  •  Rivers erod and cause parts of the river to detatch over long periods of times and create small lakes called Oxbow lakes.
  • Flood plains: Areas that flood when a river swells.

            Estuaries
  • Occur where a river flows into the ocean or an inland sea
  • Coastal estuaries are brackish ecosystems; organisms must tolerate wide salinity and temperature ranges.(Tides can cause salinity and temperature changes) 
  • Coastal estuaries are home to salt marshes(Prevents flooding, pollution, and provides habitats to oysters, turtles, etc.) and mangrove forests.
  • Like wetlands, estuaries help prevent flooding and soil erosion as well as provide habitat. 
  • Estuaries have a very high NET PRODUCTIVITY.

            Oceans
  • Currents are driven by water temperature and density differences, wind, and gravity.
  • Surface winds and heating generate vertical currents that transport nutrients and oxygen.
  • Horizontal ocean zones: Intertidal, neritic, open ocean
    • Intertidal zone: Difference between high tide and low tide
    • Neritic: Always under water, in the photic zone 
    • Open ocean: Everything else.
  • Vertical Ocean zones: Photic, Aphotic, Benthic. 

            Ocean Ecosystems
  • Intertidal: Highly diverse; extreme range of temperature, moisture, and salinity. 
  • Neritic: Productive kelp forests and coral reefs provide habitats and help protect shorelines from erosion.
  • Open Ocean: Low productivity due to low light penetration; Phytoplankton(Responsible for 50% of oxygen on the planet) base of food chain; deep sea organisms and hydrothermal vent communities.
