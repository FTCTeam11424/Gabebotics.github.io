Against Helvetians, who were invading into the fields of the neighboring people, Caesar started war. When in one day he had been able to make a bridge over Arar and led his army across, to the Helvetians, who completed the same difficult thing in twenty days, sent ambassadors to him. In such a way, they said, “If the Roman people would make peace with us, in these boundaries which you want, then we will stay. But if you have it in mind to oppress us with war for a longer time, think about our ancient reputation. For in such a way we learned from our fathers and our ancestors and we wage war more with might than with ambush. But if you should lead your army against us, then not only would you lose your salute, but also the honor of the Roman people certainly. 

     To this Caesar responded in such a way: “Even now if I were able to put down the memory of your earlier faults, then certainly I would not be able to pardon your new evils. Unless you give hostages to us, I will not make peace with you. 

    The Helvetians, if they had yielded to Caesar, then they would have found better fortune. But arrogantly they responded: “This custom is handed down to us from our ancestors: The Helvetians take hostages, not give them.”
